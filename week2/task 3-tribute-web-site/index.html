Legacy

Viktor E. Frankl’s teachings continue to inspire millions around the world. His message of finding meaning even in suffering remains timeless.

Biography

Viktor Emil Frankl (March 26, 1905, Vienna, Austria-Hungary – September 2, 1997, Vienna, Austria) was an Austrian neurologist and psychiatrist. He was the founder of logotherapy, often referred to as the “third Viennese school of psychotherapy.” He developed the method of existential analysis, which is a branch of existential psychotherapy.

One of his best-known works is the book titled ... trotzdem Ja zum Leben sagen (English: Man’s Search for Meaning), published in 1946, in which Frankl recounts his experiences during World War II in four different concentration camps, including Auschwitz, and the Holocaust.

As the most prominent figure in existential therapy, Frankl became an inspiration for a more humanistic scientific approach and a more humane psychology.

Books

Man’s Search for Meaning

Frankl’s most famous work, describing his experiences in Nazi concentration camps and his psychological insights on finding meaning in suffering.

The Unheard Cry for Meaning

A continuation of Frankl’s exploration into existential psychology and humanity’s search for purpose in modern life.

The Will to Meaning

This book expands on the principles of logotherapy and provides practical approaches to applying meaning-centered therapy.
